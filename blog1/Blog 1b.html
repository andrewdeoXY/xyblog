Syndemic Patterns of Chronic Disease Rates Across the United States¶

In our previous blog post, we looked at the rates of chronic diseases, namely coronary heart disease (CHD), cancer, and stroke, and their distributions in on the macroscale (states and counties) to the microscale, tracts within cities, in the United States. In this blog post, we’ll more closely examine the relationship between these disease rates in different geographic regions, including census tracts, cities, and states. To examine these relationships, we’ll use linear regression analysis, reporting various statistics which will give us an idea of how closely related the rates of these diseases are. Understanding these relationships, or the “correlation structure” between these chronic diseases will provide us with an idea of which diseases are closely related and tend to co-occur in the same community. It will also give us an idea of how diseases manifest: coronary heart disease is a disease of the arteries of the heart. Since stroke also affects arteries, we might expect these conditions to be closely related in that communities with high rates of CHD will have high rates of stroke.

The National Center for Chronic Disease Prevention and Health Promotion (NCCDPHP) has noted that 6 in 10 Americans have one or more chronic disease (examples including heart disease, stroke, or diabetes) [1]. Better understanding the pattern of these diseases could help health care workers better manage these diseases by identifying which conditions to test for in tandem, and how to optimize medications and prevention programs. An epidemic can be loosely defined as the widespread occurrence of a disease in a community. Multiple epidemics occurring in the same space and time, and which synergistically interact with each other, could be described as a “syndemic”. Understanding correlations can help us understand how different chronic diseases interact and identify particular chronic disease syndemics in a community. We’ll perform a series of analyses to explore these correlations and identify which chronic diseases are most closely correlated at various geographic levels (census tract, city, and state).

def ShowScatter(xval, yval, tlabels, ttitle, xtitle, ytitle):
    plt.figure()
    fig, ax1 = plt.subplots()
    (m, b) = np.polyfit(xval, yval, 1)
    correlation = np.corrcoef(xval, yval)[0,1]
    r = correlation
    tforecast = np.append(cancervals, [30])
    yp = np.polyval([m, b], tforecast)
    plt.plot(tforecast, yp, label="Regression fit: slope: " + str(m)[0:4] + " intercept: " + str(b)[0:4] + " r: " + str(r)[0:4])
    if tlabels == 'None':
        plt.scatter(xval, yval)
    if tlabels != 'None':
        plt.scatter(xval, yval, tlabels)
    plt.xlim(0, 30)
    plt.ylim(0, 30)
    plt.title(ttitle)
    plt.xlabel(xtitle)
    plt.ylabel(ytitle)
    plt.legend()
    plt.show()

# Let's take the average disease rate for each city in the 500 cities data:
finalData = defaultdict(lambda: defaultdict(float))
coordData = defaultdict(str)
cityCancer = defaultdict(float)
cityStroke = defaultdict(float)
cityCHD = defaultdict(float)
for trow in my_record:
    # we'll group by city using an identifier that includes stateabbr and placename
    tid = trow['stateabbr'] + "\t" + trow['placename']
    finalData[tid]['CancerTotal'] += trow['CANCER_CrudePrev']
    finalData[tid]['CancerCount'] += 1
    finalData[tid]['StrokeTotal'] += trow['STROKE_CrudePrev']
    finalData[tid]['StrokeCount'] += 1
    finalData[tid]['CHDTotal'] += trow['CHD_CrudePrev']
    finalData[tid]['CHDCount'] += 1
    coordData[tid] = str(trow['Geolocation'])

# and take the average:
for trow in finalData:
    cityCancer[trow] = finalData[trow]['CancerTotal'] / finalData[trow]['CancerCount']
    cityStroke[trow] = finalData[trow]['StrokeTotal'] / finalData[trow]['StrokeCount']
    cityCHD[trow] = finalData[trow]['CHDTotal'] / finalData[trow]['CHDCount']

# plot correlations by city:
df2 = pd.DataFrame({'Cancer': cityCancer, 'Stroke': cityStroke, 'CHD': cityCHD})

# plot correlations by city:
colordata = defaultdict(str)
df3 = pd.DataFrame({'Cancer': cityCancer, 'Stroke': cityStroke, 'CHD': cityCHD, 'Color': colordata})

def ScatterWithLabels(data, xval, yval, ttitle, xtitle, ytitle, xlow, xhigh, ylow, yhigh, txlimlo, txlimhi, tylimlo, tylimhi):

    # annotate each point with city name
    strokebelt = []
    strokecheck = ["AL", "AR", "FL", "GA", "KY", "LA", "MS", "NC", "OH", "PA", "SC", "TN", "TX", "VA", "WV"]
    plt.scatter(data[[xval]], data[[yval]], c="blue")
    
    for index, row in data.iterrows():
        
        # now let's pick out cities that are in stroke-belt states (the IDs are "stateabbr \t placename")
        tlabel = index
        if tlabel.find("\t") != -1:
            tdata = tlabel.split("\t")
            tlabel = tdata[1]
            if tdata[0] in strokecheck:
                strokebelt.append(index)
        
        # if the ID is just "state" for the analysis below...
        if tlabel.find("\t") == -1:
            if index in strokecheck:
                strokebelt.append(index)
            
        if (xlow == 0) | ((row[xval] < xlow) | (row[xval] >= xhigh)) | ((row[yval] < ylow) | (row[yval] >= yhigh)):
            plt.text(row[xval] * (1 + 0.005), row[yval] * (1 + 0.005) , tlabel, fontsize=14)

    # highlight stroke belt
    rowData = data.loc[strokebelt, : ]
    plt.scatter(rowData[[xval]], rowData[[yval]], c="red")

    plt.xlim(txlimlo, txlimhi)
    plt.ylim(tylimlo, tylimhi)
    plt.title(ttitle)
    plt.xlabel(xtitle)
    plt.ylabel(ytitle)
    plt.rcParams["figure.figsize"] = (15,15)
    plt.show()
    
ScatterWithLabels(df3, 'CHD', 'Stroke', 'Title', 'CHD Rate', 'Stroke Rate', 3, 8, 2, 4, 0, 15, 0, 10)

From the city plot above, the same correlation pattern can be seen, with CHD correlating the strongest with stroke, and the weakest correlations seen between cancer and stroke.

Part 4 - Differences in Disease Rates¶

Now that we have looked at the relationship in disease rates, let's look at how the differences in disease rates between 2013-2015 are correlated with each other. First, we'll extract data for 2015 and subtract it from the 2013 data:

# Now subtract the 2015-2016 data (in datafrom2016) from our 2013 data (finalData):

chdDiff = defaultdict(float)
for trow in datafrom2016:

    finalData[tid]['CancerCount'] += 1
    finalData[tid]['StrokeTotal'] += trow['STROKE_CrudePrev'] - cityStroke[tid]
    finalData[tid]['StrokeCount'] += 1
    finalData[tid]['CHDTotal'] += trow['CHD_CrudePrev'] - cityCHD[tid]
    finalData[tid]['CHDCount'] += 1
    
# and take the average:
for trow in finalData:
    cancerDiff[trow] = finalData[trow]['CancerTotal'] / finalData[trow]['CancerCount']
    strokeDiff[trow] = finalData[trow]['StrokeTotal'] / finalData[trow]['StrokeCount']
    chdDiff[trow] = finalData[trow]['CHDTotal'] / finalData[trow]['CHDCount']

Conclusions¶

We were able to visualize the correlations between different chronic diseases at the census tract, city, and state levels, noting that similar patterns of correlation exist at every geographic level. By plotting the change in disease rates, we can also identify which cities are doing well at improving the health of their population, and which cities face challenges.
